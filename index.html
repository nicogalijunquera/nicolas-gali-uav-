Project Overview

Why Longer Wings Make Better Drones

In plain terms

Think of an albatross versus a sparrow — the long, narrow wings of the albatross waste almost no energy in flight. The same principle applies to drone wings. This project quantifies exactly how much efficiency is gained by increasing the wing's aspect ratio, and confirms the structure can still handle competition loads.

Project Context & Motivation ▾

The Brunel UAS Society's 2022 competition wing (AR = 6.3, NACA 4415) is the starting baseline. The IMechE UAS Challenge 2026 rewards efficiency, endurance and technical depth — all areas where a higher aspect ratio configuration offers a quantifiable scoring advantage.

Research objective: Quantify the L/D gain from increasing AR on a fixed-area wing (S ≈ 1.0 m²), and confirm structural viability under 3.5 G competition loads.

The Physics — Aspect Ratio & Induced Drag ▾

Induced drag scales inversely with aspect ratio — doubling AR roughly halves the induced drag term. This is the fundamental aerodynamic motivation for the project.

C_D = C_d + C_L² / (π · e · AR) M_root ∝ √AR → +26% at AR = 10

Methodology

How the Study Was Conducted

The approach

Three methods combined: virtual wind tunnel simulations (CFD) to predict aerodynamic performance, adjoint-based shape optimisation to refine the aerofoil profile, and analytical beam theory to verify structural integrity — all validated before conclusions were drawn.

CFD Setup — ANSYS Fluent ▾

RANS simulations with SST k-ω turbulence model. Both 2D (aerofoil) and 3D (full wing) domains simulated. Wall-resolved meshes with y⁺ ≈ 1 — no wall functions.

Aerofoil Optimisation — Adjoint Method ▾

The adjoint method (ANSYS Fluent, [9]) computes drag sensitivity across every surface point simultaneously using just two CFD solves — far more efficient than finite-difference approaches for large numbers of design variables.

  • Starting profile: NACA 4415
  • Objective: minimise drag, maintain lift
  • Primary change at lower trailing edge — reduces adverse pressure gradient
  • 2D optimisation applied to 3D wing
  • Gain: +2.38% L/D
Winglet Design — Modular Plug-in ▾

Winglets disrupt the wingtip vortex — a direct energy loss — and recover it as useful lift-to-drag improvement. Designed as a modular plug-in to the wingtip rib for competition transport practicality.

  • Gain: +4.05% L/D
  • AReff = 10.34 (CFD equivalence) — preferred over geometric estimate of 10.75
  • Removable without structural modification to main wing

The winglet visibly weakens the tip vortex, recovering energy that would otherwise be permanently lost to the wake.

Structural Analysis — Rib Sizing ▾

AR increase from 6.3 to 10 raises the root bending moment by +26% (√AR scaling, Megson [8]). Analytical beam theory used to size ribs under 3.5 G loading with an elliptic lift distribution.

q(y) = q₀ × √(1 − (2y/b)²) V(0) = 67.5 N (root shear force)

Results

Performance Across All Five Configurations

The outcome

Five wing designs tested in full 3D CFD, each isolating one change. The final configuration (Model 5) achieves an L/D of 21.66 versus 16.99 for the baseline — a substantial gain through three additive, well-characterised optimisation steps.

Conclusions & Future Work ▾
  • AR increase 6.3 → 10 is the dominant driver (+19.99%); aerofoil and winglet deliver additive gains.
  • Structural viability confirmed with targeted reinforcement at just 2 rib positions.
  • Modular winglet satisfies competition transport constraints.
  • AReff = 10.34 (CFD) preferred over geometric estimate of 10.75.
Future work: 3D adjoint optimisation (estimated 2–3× higher gain), wind tunnel validation, full FEA, and physical prototype + flight test.
